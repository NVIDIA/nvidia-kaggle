I spent a good chunk of time reviewing the literature for this competition      
before writing a single line of code. This post is that review, structured as a 
progressive reading guide — from the geological basics to state-of-the-art deep 
learning.                                                                       

The philosophy here: understanding why an algorithm works on this data is more  
valuable than blindly throwing models at it.                                    

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

                   1. The Physical Problem (Don't Skip This)                    

Before any algorithm, let's be clear on what we're actually trying to do.       

When a horizontal well is drilled, the drill bit travels along geological layers
rather than cutting through them vertically. This creates a fundamental         
ambiguity: at any given MD (Measured Depth — the cable length along the         
borehole), where exactly are we in the rock pile?                               

This is what TVT answers. In this competition specifically, TVT is not a        
classical layer thickness — it's a relative geological depth index: how far     
vertically are we from a reference surface that follows the local geological    
structure.                                                                      

The three depth coordinates:                                                    

 • MD: length along the borehole trajectory. The only coordinate tools actually 
   measure.                                                                     
 • TVD = $\int_0^{MD} \cos(I), dMD$ — true vertical depth. Straightforward      
   geometry.                                                                    
 • TVT: corrects for both borehole geometry and formation dip. This is what     
   tells you "you're 15 ft above the Buda limestone" regardless of how the well 
   meanders.                                                                    

                       The Gamma Ray as a GPS in the rock                       

GR measures natural radioactivity (K, U, Th) in API units. The key physical     
fact:                                                                           

 • Shales concentrate radioactive minerals during deposition → high GR (> 100   
   API)                                                                         
 • Carbonates / clean sandstones are chemically inert → low GR (< 40 API)       

This means each geological formation has a characteristic GR "fingerprint". In  
the Texas Eagle Ford play targeted here, the stratigraphy reads like a barcode:

The typewell is a vertical reference well that crossed all these layers in one  
clean pass. It provides the canonical GR barcode for the area.                  

The core problem: the lateral well's GR log is a deformed version of this       
barcode — stretched, compressed, shifted — because the well meanders along the  
layers rather than cutting through them. Our job is to continuously match the   
deformed barcode against the reference to read the stratigraphic position (TVT).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

         2. Dynamic Time Warping — The Right Tool for Deformed Signals          

                      Why classic correlation fails first                       

The naive approach is a simple cross-correlation: slide the lateral GR window   
over the typewell GR and find the best match. This works for a rigid shift (the 
same sequence, just offset). But real geology doesn't work that way:            

 • A limestone bed might be 5 ft thick near one well and 12 ft thick 2 km away  
   (lateral thickness variations due to compaction, paleotopography...)         
 • The drill bit may have spent more time in one layer if the well undulates    

The lateral GR signal is non-linearly stretched relative to the typewell.       
Classic correlation produces garbage alignments in these cases.                 

                             DTW: elastic alignment                             

DTW (Sakoe & Chiba, 1978) — originally designed for speech recognition — solves 
exactly this. The insight: instead of finding a single shift, find the optimal  
path through a 2D cost matrix that warps both signals onto each other.          

$$D(i,j) = c(x_i, y_j) + \min{D(i-1,j),; D(i,j-1),; D(i-1,j-1)}$$               

where $c(x_i, y_j) = |x_i - y_j|$ is the local cost (GR difference). The path   
must start at $(1,1)$ and end at $(N,M)$, cannot go backwards (monotonicity     
constraint), and is found in $O(N \times M)$ by dynamic programming.            

▌ Key intuition: DTW lets one point in the lateral log "match" multiple points
▌ in the typewell (or vice versa). That's the elastic stretching. A thin layer
▌ in the lateral that corresponds to a thick layer in the typewell is handled 
▌ naturally.                                                                  

                        Critical constraints for geology                        

Without constraints, DTW is prone to degenerate alignments: a single point      
matching an entire section. Two classical fixes:                                

Sakoe-Chiba band — only allow alignments within $w$ samples of the diagonal:    
$$|i - j| \leq w$$ This reduces complexity from $O(N^2)$ to $O(w \cdot N)$ and  
prevents geologically absurd matchings (e.g. aligning an 11,000 ft TVT layer    
with a 9,000 ft layer).                                                         

Itakura parallelogram — constrains path slopes to $[1/2, 2]$, forbidding extreme
compression or expansion. Appropriate when the geology is structurally simple   
and layer thickness variations are bounded.                                     

                         Where vanilla DTW breaks down                          

 • Faults / missing rock: the monotonicity condition ($i_{k-1} \leq i_k$) means 
   DTW cannot model repeated stratigraphy (reverse faults) or missing sections. 
   You need external domain knowledge to detect and handle these.               
 • Error accumulation: without external anchor points, alignment errors drift in
   multi-well correlation loops (the loop doesn't close to zero).               
 • Open boundary problem: a lateral well rarely starts at the exact top of the  
   typewell sequence. Use Subsequence DTW (free start/end conditions on one     
   side) to handle this.                                                        

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

             3. Signal Correlation Methods — From Rigid to Elastic              

A useful mental model: think of these three methods as progressively more       
flexible alignment tools.                                                       

    3.1 Classical Cross-Correlation — "Does it look the same if I shift it?"    

$$(f \star g)[\tau] = \sum_t f[t], g[t + \tau]$$                                

Slides $g$ over $f$, measures overlap. Fast, simple, but amplitude-sensitive:   
different LWD tools or borehole diameters shift absolute GR values              
systematically, creating false alignment peaks. Use only as a very rough first  
pass.                                                                           

    3.2 Normalized Cross-Correlation (NCC) — "Does it have the same shape?"     

$$NCC(\tau) = \frac{\sum_t (f[t]-\bar{f})(g[t+\tau]-\bar{g})}                   
{\sqrt{\sum_t(f[t]-\bar{f})^2 \cdot \sum_t(g[t+\tau]-\bar{g})^2}}$$             

This is the Pearson correlation coefficient computed on a sliding window. Result
is always in $[-1, 1]$, completely amplitude-invariant: if the lateral GR tool  
reads 20% higher than the typewell tool due to borehole conditions, NCC is      
unaffected.                                                                     

▌ Practical rule of thumb: a sliding NCC score > 0.85 on a 30 m window is a   
▌ high-confidence stratigraphic anchor point. NCC won't tell you about        
▌ stretching, but it tells you "this wiggle in the lateral GR is definitely   
▌ this wiggle in the typewell, with high confidence".                         

In this dataset, we computed NCC scores across 100 wells: the median peak NCC is
~0.81, with ~31% of wells showing at least one anchor point above 0.75.         

        3.3 Phase Correlation — "What's the exact shift, even in noise?"        

$$\Phi =                                                                        
\mathcal{F}^{-1}!\left(\frac{F(\omega),G^(\omega)}{|F(\omega),G^(\omega)|}\right
)$$                                                                             

Operates entirely in the frequency domain. If $g$ is a shifted copy of $f$, the 
result $\Phi$ is a Dirac impulse at the exact offset — not a smooth correlation 
peak. This gives sub-sample precision and is robust to broadband noise (LWD tool
background noise cancels out during normalization).                             

The limitation: same as classical cross-correlation, assumes a rigid shift. Best
used to detect coarse offset and faults at macro scale, before handing off to   
DTW for local elastic alignment.

The Soft-DTW in tslearn deserves a special mention. Standard DTW uses a $\min$  
operator which is not differentiable — you can't backpropagate through it.      
Soft-DTW (Cuturi & Blondel, 2017) replaces $\min$ with a smooth approximation   
$\min_\gamma$, making the alignment loss trainable end-to-end in a neural       
network. This matters if you want to embed DTW directly in a loss function (e.g.
to penalize stratigraphic misalignment in a LSTM or Transformer).               

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

                 5. Can Foundation Models Help? A Critical Look                 

The obvious question after reading about DTW and NCC: "Can we just throw a      
pretrained time series model at this and let it figure it out?"                 

The answer is: maybe, but not the way you'd expect. Here's why.                 

               What Foundation Models for Time Series actually do               

Over the past 2 years, several large pretrained models have emerged that work on
time series the same way LLMs work on text: train on billions of diverse        
sequences, then zero-shot forecast on new data.                                 

The critical translation for this problem: our independent variable is depth    
(MD), not time. We're doing spatial inference along a borehole, not temporal    
forecasting. That changes how these models should be used.                      

Also — and this is the key insight — we're not predicting GR. We're estimating a
hidden state variable (TVT) from an observed signal (GR) given a reference      
(typewell). That's not a forecasting task, it's more like a Bayesian state      
estimation problem. This means these models should ideally be used as feature   
extractors (encoder representations → regression head), not as direct           
forecasters.

                        Why Patching matters for GR logs                        

A single GR measurement at one depth point carries almost no geological         
information on its own — it's dominated by tool noise and borehole effects. The 
geological signal lives in the shape of the curve over 5–15 ft: a               
peak-trough-peak pattern identifies a formation boundary, a flat plateau        
identifies a homogeneous rock mass.                                             

Patch-based models (PatchTST, TimesFM) encode the input as a sequence of local  
windows ("patches") rather than individual points — exactly matching the scale  
at which geology expresses itself in a GR log. This is not accidental: it's the 
right inductive bias for this type of data.                                     

                          Why Chronos scores low here                           

Chronos tokenizes the time series by normalizing and quantizing values into a   
discrete vocabulary (like converting a signal into "words"). This works         
beautifully for macroscopic trends in economic or climate data.                 

But in a GR log, a 1 m thin carbonate interlayer — a potential key stratigraphic
marker — appears as a brief high-amplitude spike. Quantization smooths or       
misclassifies this spike, potentially erasing the very feature that pins the    
stratigraphic correlation.                                                      

                          The XReg advantage (TimesFM)                          

This competition is fundamentally spatial: the same TVT can correspond to very  
different GR values depending on location (X, Y, Z), lateral thickness          
variations, and structural dip. Igor Kuvaev (ROGII) confirmed in the forum that 
XYZ coordinates carry critical spatial information that GR alone cannot resolve.

TimesFM's XReg feature allows passing exogenous regressors (X, Y, Z,            
inclination, surface depths from nearby wells) alongside the GR sequence as     
conditioning inputs. That's exactly the architecture we need.                   

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

                      6. A Few Things the Forum Made Clear                      

A direct quote from Igor Kuvaev (ROGII organizer) paraphrased:                  

▌ "XYZ of lateral wells = true spatial position. The typewell = vertical      
▌ geological definition (GR as a function of TVT). Connecting the two is the  
▌ problem."                                                                   

Some practical observations from top participants and organizers:               

 • Naive tabular XGBoost on [X, Y, Z, MD, GR] hits a glass ceiling around ~9.7  
   public LB. It ignores the physical trajectory of the well.                   
 • Geological layers are spatially continuous. A test well missing surface depth
   data can have it imputed from its 10 nearest geographical neighbors with very
   high accuracy (R² > 0.99 in cross-validation on the training set).           
 • Sequential context matters: the 50 points just before the PS (Prediction     
   Start) contain the local slope, GR trend, and last known TVT — the           
   highest-value features for immediate prediction.
